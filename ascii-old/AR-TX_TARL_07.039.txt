INTRODUCTION
On June 27, 1980, at the request of the Lower Colorado
River Authority, an archeological survey of a 1.5 mile long,
85 foot wide proposed transmission corridor was conducted by
Bruce A. Nightengale, staff member of the Texas Archeological
Survey. The corridor extends north 2,000 feet (610 m.) from
a substation south of Piney Creek, then northeast 6,000 feet
(1.75 k.) to intersect with an existing transmission line.
The transmission corridor traverses different geographic
zones which are characterized by various vegetation types and
prior land use: floodplains whose elevation averages about 350
feet AMSL; valley margins; and gently rolling uplands with a
maximum elevation of 425 feet. Veget~t;on ranges from Bermuda
grass in cultivated pasture to weeds and grasses in fallow
fields. Pine, oak and elm trees fringe the tributary streams.
Soil matrix varies with the geographic locale. In the
floodplains and pastures sandy loams overlie clay loams. In
the uplands and drainages the soils are fine sandy loams over­
lying sandy clay (Soil Survey Staff 1979).
RESULTS OF THE STUDY
An examination of the archives of the Texas Archeological
Research Laboratory, the University of Texas at Austin, revealed
no sites have been recorded in the transmission corridor. Sites
listed for the general area result from the work of both amateur
and professional archeologists. In and around the city of Bastrop,
on the southern periphery of the transmission corridor, numerous
sites are recorded. To the northeast, a cultural resource inventory
and assessment of Camp Swift was carried out by the Texas
Archeological Survey last year. Forty two prehistoric and forty
three historic sites were recorded in the 4,000 acre survey area.
(Skelton and Freeman 1979).

Also recently, Site 41BP 89 which lies on a sandy upland
knoll about two kilometers west of the present survey area,
was excavated by the Travis County Archeological Society but
is as of yet unreported (see Skelton and Prewitt 1978).
The pedestrian survey of the transmission corridor
consisted of zig-zag transects walked from one boundary of
the corridor to the other and measured from the center line.
Two prehistoric sites were located - 41BP197, Areas A and B,
and 41BP198. (See attached site survey forms). One historic
trash dump was noted eroding from the southern cut bank of
Piney Creek approximately 60 meters north of the LCRA sub­
station. Material consisted of medicine bottles dating from
the early 1900·s (Jackson, personal communication) and various
fragments of earthenware and tin. Most of this material is
considered to be discarded during the first half of the
twentieth century. Also throughout the survey area, large
quantities of chert cobbles were evident in outcroppings
along the line. These naturally occurring lithic resources
were generally unmodified.
The site areas were delineated with flagging tape and
on June 30, 1980 the construction personnel were taken to
the sites so that, if at all possible, construction activities
could be routed around these sites. Avoidance was considered
the most feasible method of preventing damage or disruption of
the cultural deposits.
The LCRA construction personnel intended to remove the
trees within the right-of-way by hand-cutting. However, the
landowner at Site 41BP198 requested no stumps be left on his
property. Therefore, on July 7, the bulldozer clearing of
trees from the northeastern edge of this site was monitored.
Approximately 15 cm. of the surface was removed. No cultural
material was exposed by the uprooted trees or in the surface
scraping.
No further recommendations for work at these sites is
warranted unless they will be directly affected by tower
construction.
